Understanding Riker's World

What I've Learned About Sensory-Movement Experience in Autism

The Core Realization

Autism isn't just about sensory processing or social differences. It's a sensory-MOVEMENT disability. And understanding that changes everything about how we interpret what Riker's doing.

Part 1: The Sensory Piece (What You Already Suspected)

Hyposensitivity = Seeking More Input

You were right about the psychedelic comparison. Research and first-person accounts confirm that autistic sensory processing creates genuinely different experiences of reality.

What "hyposensitive" actually means:

  • Normal sensory input doesn't reach threshold for full processing
  • Brain isn't filtering OUT too much (like hypersensitivity) - it's requiring MORE to register
  • Seeking behaviors aren't random - they're pursuing specific sensory states

    • The metal lunchbox makes perfect sense:

  • Loud, resonant sound = finally enough auditory input
  • Vibration through hands/body = proprioceptive feedback (body awareness)
  • Repetitive = creates predictable pattern in chaotic sensory field
  • Makes him happy = achieves the regulation state his nervous system needs

    • From autistic adults who were sensory-seekers as kids: "I might self-stimulate by running my fingertips, repetitively, across a rough patch of carpet, so that I can stop every once in a while and delight in how numb they have become."

    The seeking isn't about more-more-more endlessly. It's about reaching a specific threshold where sensation finally becomes processable, organizing, even pleasurable.

    Part 2: The Movement Piece (The Missing Half)

    This Is Where It Gets Really Important

    Sensing = INPUT. Moving = OUTPUT.

    Both controlled by the same cranial nerve system. If sensory processing is fundamentally different, motor functioning will be too.

    What researchers discovered: When they compared "movement disturbances recognized as neurological in non-autistic people" to "behaviors labeled as intentional in autistic people" - they're the same things.

    Examples from the research:

  • "Repetitive motor actions" (neurological) = "stimming" (behavioral label)
  • "Difficulty with stopping/cessation of movement" (neurological) = "obsessive behavior" (behavioral label)
  • "Requires prompts and cues to perform" (neurological) = "lazy/non-compliant" (behavioral label)

    • This is HUGE for understanding Riker.

    Part 3: The Lunchbox Makes Even More Sense Now

    Why He Can Drum for 30-60 Minutes

    It's not just sensory seeking. It's motor functioning.

    Autistic people often have: 1. Difficulty initiating movement - hard to start 2. Difficulty stopping movement - hard to stop once started 3. Motor patterns that persist - movements continue unless externally redirected

    What this means for the lunchbox:

  • Starting the drumming might actually be easier than starting OTHER movements (predictable, repetitive)
  • Once started, the motor pattern is self-sustaining
  • Stopping requires external intervention (running out of steam, you redirecting him)
  • The repetition itself might be organizing his motor system
  • He's getting BOTH sensory regulation AND motor regulation simultaneously

    • It's not "he won't stop" - it's "neurologically difficult to stop."

    Part 4: The Visual Fixation (Toy Manipulation)

    Turning Objects Over and Over for an Hour

    What might be happening: 1. Visual processing: Seeing patterns/details/relationships that aren't obvious to neurotypical perception 2. Motor sustaining: Same object, same action = sustainable motor pattern 3. Proprioceptive feedback: Hand/arm movement providing body awareness input 4. Integration: Processing how visual input relates to motor output in a predictable loop

    The psychedelic comparison holds: If edges look more pronounced, textures seem to move, colors/patterns are enhanced - then turning the same toy reveals NEW visual information each time. It's not repetitive to him. It's continuous discovery.

    And the motor piece: His neurology supports sustained, repetitive motor patterns better than complex, varied ones. So studying one object intensely is more accessible than manipulating many objects briefly.

    Why Speech Is So Hard

    Speech is the most complex motor function humans do.

    It requires:

  • Precise coordination of tongue, lips, vocal cords, breath
  • Split-second timing
  • Integration with auditory processing (hearing yourself)
  • Connection to language centers in brain
  • Voluntary initiation AND stopping

    • For someone with motor differences like Riker: The same neurology that makes starting/stopping drumming hard makes speech EXPONENTIALLY harder. It's not that he has nothing to say. It's that the motor output system for speech is incredibly demanding.

    This doesn't mean he's not thinking.

    Research suggests many autistic people think in:

  • Images and visual patterns
  • Sensory associations
  • Concepts without words
  • Spatial relationships

    • Riker might be organizing his world through:

  • The visual patterns in the toys he studies
  • The proprioceptive feedback from drumming
  • The sensory states he creates and returns to
  • Movement patterns that make sense to his neurology

    • His thinking might not NEED language the way yours does.
